Research approach 01

How this analysis was conducted

This teardown draws on three sources, each with different signal types. No internal Razorpay data was available — all claims are either directly sourced from public statements and published research, or explicitly flagged as hypotheses.

First-hand merchant onboarding
Signed up as an SME and went through the full Payment Pages setup — including KYC, business verification, and the product builder. Observations are based on direct experience of the flow.
Merchant review analysis
Read approximately 120 merchant reviews across Capterra, Trustpilot, and the Shopify App Store. Complaints were categorised by theme. The distribution of themes — and the notable absence of Payment Pages feedback — forms a key input into this analysis. [see refs 4–6]
Public statements and market data
CEO interview from March 2026 (Business Today), Razorpay's own published statistics, IAMAI Digital India data, and published research on Indian SMB commerce behaviour. All sourced in the references section.
Strategic context 02

Razorpay's stated direction: from payment gateway to financial OS

In March 2026, Razorpay's co-founder and CEO Harshil Mathur spoke publicly about the company's ambition to expand beyond its core payments business. This is the context in which Payment Pages becomes a strategically interesting product to examine — not as a standalone feature, but as a potential entry point into a much larger merchant relationship.

"The idea is to evolve Razorpay beyond a payments provider into a financial operating system for businesses — with the biggest impact possible among SMEs, which often lack dedicated teams to manage even basic payment-related processes."

Harshil Mathur, Co-founder & CEO, Razorpay [ref 1] ↗

The scale at which Razorpay operates makes the SME layer a meaningful strategic surface. Razorpay's merchant base grew from 350,000 in 2019 to over 10 million by 2023 — with a significant share of transaction volume coming from smaller merchants outside Tier 1 cities.

[observed pattern across ref 4, 5, 6]
Hypothesis on the review signal
The near-absence of Payment Pages feedback suggests the feature is not currently competing in the space where merchants evaluate storefront products. Merchants building serious online stores appear to use Shopify or WooCommerce and treat Razorpay as the payment layer. The opportunity cost of this pattern is difficult to quantify from public data, but it represents a structural gap in how Razorpay captures SME value beyond the transaction.
SME commerce behaviour 04

Where Indian SMEs actually sell today

A meaningful proportion of Indian small sellers do not maintain a formal website. Their primary commerce channels are WhatsApp catalogues, Instagram product posts, and word-of-mouth referrals — with payment collected via UPI or a payment link sent through chat.

First-hand product observations 05

What the current onboarding experience looks like

The following observations come from going through the SME sign-up and Payment Pages setup as a new merchant. These are product-level findings, not judgements about Razorpay's broader quality or engineering — the core payment infrastructure is clearly robust based on both usage and the review data above.

Observation 1: Value preview is gated behind full KYC
Before a new merchant can see what their store will look like, the onboarding flow requires business type, GST details, bank account, and PAN. There is no sandbox or preview mode. Platforms like Shopify and Wix both allow merchants to build and preview a full store before entering payment or compliance details — deferring commitment until the product has demonstrated value. The current Razorpay flow inverts this sequence.
Observation 2: The builder is scoped to payment collection, not storefront creation
The Payment Pages builder surfaces fields for product name, image, and price. This is appropriate for its current positioning as a checkout-link tool — but it does not support the elements that drive conversion on a seller's first impression: brand identity, trust signals, delivery information, or an about section. The product is completing one job (collect payment) while leaving the adjacent job (earn buyer confidence) unaddressed.
Observation 3: Magic Checkout is a strong and underutilised trust asset
As a repeat consumer across multiple D2C brands, Magic Checkout — prefilled address, saved card details, single-tap payment — is noticeably smoother than alternatives. Razorpay's stated network of 40 crore+ users with saved details is a genuine consumer trust asset. Currently, this trust signal activates only at the payment step. Surfacing it earlier in the buyer journey — at the storefront level, before a purchase decision — is an unrealised opportunity.
Competitive context 06

Where the market has moved

AI-generated storefront setup is no longer a product concept — it has shipped in the global market. The comparison below is not to suggest Razorpay should replicate these products, but to establish that the category exists and that the India-native version of it remains unbuilt.

Platform AI setup India SME fit Payment-first
Shopify Magic [ref 9] Describe business → full storefront generated including layout and product copy Global product. USD pricing. Not optimised for UPI or Tier 2/3 India merchants. Razorpay integration is an add-on, not native
Wix Aria [ref 10] Conversational AI setup, brand kit auto-generated, mobile-optimised layout Global product. No UPI-first checkout. INR pricing not primary. Requires separate payment gateway integration
Instamojo No-code store builder. Manual setup. No AI assistance currently. India-native. Built for freelancers and small sellers. Payment links central to the product. Own payment gateway. Smaller merchant trust network than Razorpay.
Razorpay Payment Pages Manual setup. Product + price fields. No brand builder. India-native. UPI-first. Highest merchant trust network. Magic Checkout built in. Best-in-class at the payment moment.
The combination no competitor currently has
Shopify and Wix have AI storefront generation but are not India-native. Instamojo is India-native but does not have AI setup or Razorpay's trust network. Razorpay is the only player with a UPI-first payment infrastructure, a 40 crore+ consumer network with saved checkout details, and an existing relationship with 10M+ merchants. The India-specific, AI-native storefront product sits in a gap that none of these platforms currently fills.
Product opportunities 07

Three directions worth exploring

These are framed as hypotheses based on the observations above, not prescriptions. Each would require validation with actual merchant behaviour data that is not publicly available.

1
AI-assisted store setup from a single prompt
Allow a merchant to describe their business in one or two sentences — what they sell, who their customers are, what price range — and generate a ready-to-publish Payment Page: brand colours, product description template, homepage copy, and category structure. Magic Checkout embedded by default. This mirrors what Shopify Magic has shipped globally, built around India's UPI context and Tier 2/3 price points.
High priority hypothesis
2
Surface Magic Checkout trust at the storefront, not only at checkout
When a buyer with saved Razorpay details lands on a Payment Page, displaying "Your details are saved — check out in one tap" before they add to cart converts the checkout trust into storefront confidence. This is a low-complexity change that makes Razorpay's largest consumer-facing moat visible at the moment it can most influence a purchase decision.
High priority hypothesis
3
WhatsApp catalogue sync
Given that 78% of Indian SMBs already operate on WhatsApp Business, a Payment Page that auto-syncs to a seller's WhatsApp catalogue — and generates a shareable payment link per product — reduces the switching cost of adopting a new channel. The seller does not need to rebuild their catalogue; they extend it.
Medium priority hypothesis
Success metrics 08

How success would be measured

If the above hypotheses were pursued, the following metrics would determine whether they're solving the right problem. These are not claimed outcomes — they're the indicators that would validate or invalidate the direction.

North star
% of newly activated merchants who make their first sale through a Razorpay-hosted storefront (rather than a standalone payment link)
Activation
Time from merchant sign-up to first live product page — target under 10 minutes with AI-assisted setup
Retention signal
Merchant retention at 30/60/90 days for storefront users vs. payment-link-only users — tests the LTV hypothesis

The underlying hypothesis is that merchants who invest in a storefront — even a minimal one — have higher long-term value than those using only payment links, because the storefront signals a sustained intent to sell rather than a one-time collection. If validated, improving storefront activation has a direct commercial rationale for Razorpay beyond feature completeness.

Summary

Razorpay's CEO has publicly described the ambition to become a financial operating system for Indian businesses. The SME segment already drives the majority of transaction volume. Payment Pages — the product most directly positioned as the storefront entry point for this segment — currently competes below its potential. In a market where AI-generated storefronts have shipped globally and 78% of Indian SMBs sell through WhatsApp, there is a specific, India-native version of this product that no competitor is currently positioned to build as credibly as Razorpay.
